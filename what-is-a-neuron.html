What is a Neuron?

The basic cell that carries information through the nervous system.

A neuron, or nerve cell, is the basic functional unit of the nervous system. Every thought, movement, sensation, and memory depends on billions of neurons communicating with one another.

Like most cells, neurons contain a nucleus, mitochondria, and other structures needed for survival. However, neurons also possess specialized structures called dendrites and axons that allow them to receive and transmit information.

Neurons are electrically excitable, meaning they can generate and carry signals. They communicate with other cells through structures called synapses. Synapses can be electrical or chemical and allow information to pass from one cell to another.

WHY ARE THEY ESSENTIAL?

Neurons are specialised cells that make us capable of activities, ranging from sleeping to parkour. Without them we would be unable to process any input from the world around us, and without any input, our brain would be unable to create output.

If our brain did not form any output, we would be unable to contribute to our world.

One thing that makes our brain dynamical is that neurons not only take imput, but also interact with other neurons to form synapses. As they are synaptically plastic, we can partially (if not fully) alter which memories and skill remain active and dominant. This is a know phenomena stated in the Hebb's law, where the most used synapses are likely to preserved longer and the synapses not used at all can lose connectivity while still being present in our mind.

HOW EXACTLY DO NEURONS COMMUNICATE?

Neurons are made up of three key components:- I. Dendrites These are branch-like structure that pick up signals from neighbouring cells. II. Soma (Cell body) The neuron's control center, that processes the signals and contains the nucleus. III. Axon A long, cable-like extension that carries the action potential away from the cell toward other cells.

1. Electrical Signaling (Within the Neuron) Information travels from one end of a single neuron to the other via an electrical impulse known as an action potential. The Resting State: At rest, the inside of a neuron carries a negative electrical charge compared to the outside. This state is maintained by an uneven balance of charged particles (ions) like sodium and potassium. The Spark: When a neuron receives a strong enough signal, gates in its cell membrane open. Positively charged sodium ions rush into the cell, rapidly flipping the charge inside from negative to positive. The Wave: This charge reversal creates an electrical wave that zips down the length of the cell’s long "tail" (the axon). 2. Chemical Signaling (Between Neurons) Neurons don't physically touch one another. They are separated by a microscopic gap called a synapse (or synaptic cleft). The electrical wave cannot jump this gap, so it must be converted into a chemical message. Release: When the electrical action potential reaches the very end of the axon, it triggers the release of specialized chemicals called neurotransmitters (like dopamine, serotonin, or glutamate). Crossing the Gap: These chemical messengers are released from storage sacs and float across the microscopic gap.Reception: Once across, they bind to specific "mailbox" proteins called receptors on the receiving neuron. The Result: Depending on the type of neurotransmitter that binds, this action can either excite the next neuron (encouraging it to pass the message along) or inhibit it (telling it to stay quiet).
